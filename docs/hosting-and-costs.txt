Hosting and costs - a quick rundown

I wanted to put down in plain terms what it costs to run ParadePaard online, and why, so we're all on the same page before we decide anything.

The main thing to understand is that hosting the app is a bit like renting a shop that stays open around the clock. You pay for the space whether five people or five hundred walk in, right up until it gets so busy that you'd need a bigger place. Our shop is almost never busy. We have around 2,500 users on paper, but nearly all of them barely show up. Most work only a handful of times a year, some once a month or once a week, and only about a hundred are full-timers, plus our five admins who are on it every day. Even at the busiest moment there are maybe a hundred to a hundred and fifty people using it at once, which a modest setup handles without breaking a sweat.

Because of that, the bill is mostly a flat monthly cost rather than something that grows with how many users we have. Someone who logs in a few times a year effectively costs us nothing extra. A monthly or weekly user costs a few cents at most. Even a very active full-timer or admin only adds a little. So 2,500 mostly quiet users cost us about the same as a few hundred would. What we're really paying for is the always-on space, a safe home for the data, and backups, not the number of names on the list.

What we actually pay for comes down to three things. The biggest is the server itself, which is the always-on computer that runs the software. The second is the database, which is where all the payroll and personal information lives, and it's the part most worth paying to keep safe. The third is the small stuff: automatic backups, which are basically insurance against losing data, the web address, and the security certificate that gives people the little padlock in their browser.

There are three sensible ways to go. The cheapest is a single server where we look after the data ourselves, and that runs somewhere around 35 to 90 euros a month. It's perfectly fine while we're small, as long as we stay disciplined about doing the backups. The middle option adds a professional, managed database that backs itself up automatically, which takes the scariest risk off our plate, and comes to roughly 130 to 220 euros a month. The top option uses a big cloud provider like AWS with a spare that takes over instantly if something fails. That's the most reliable, and it lands around 250 to 400 euros a month. All three keep the data inside the EU, which we're legally required to do for payroll and personal information, and all three include encryption and backups.

For where we are right now, I'd lean towards the middle option. The cheap one is genuinely fine if we commit to handling backups properly, and the top one is more reliability than our current traffic actually needs, but the middle sits in a comfortable, low-worry spot at a reasonable price.

A fair question is what happens if we land a new client company and suddenly have another thousand-plus users. The honest answer is that it barely moves the bill. Those users would be mostly quiet too, so they don't really add to the crowd. We'd maybe pay a little more for a slightly bigger database, not jump to the next tier. The app already keeps each client company's data separate, so taking on a new client is a setup job, not a rebuild, and we don't need new hosting for each one.

The last thing worth saying is that we're not locked in. The app was built to be easy to move, which was a big part of the recent groundwork. If we outgrow the cheaper option, or a big client wants top-tier reliability, shifting over to something like AWS is mostly a matter of copying the data across and pointing the web address at the new home. That's usually a day or a few of work, done during a short planned downtime. So the sensible plan is to start affordable now and step up later if and when it's worth it, without much pain when we do.

Short version: the cost is driven by keeping the system running and the data safe, not by how many people sign up. Around 130 to 220 euros a month covers us comfortably today, or 35 to 90 if we go lean, more users barely change that, and moving up to AWS later is straightforward.
